# Pantry Panic Blog Writing Guide

## Purpose

Every article must serve at least one of these goals:

1. SEO Capture search traffic around grocery lists, meal planning, shopping habits, family
   organization, self-hosting, PWAs, privacy, subscriptions and modern software.
2. Brand Building Reinforce Pantry Panic as the anti-bloat grocery app. Simple, fast, opinionated,
   practical and slightly rebellious.
3. Social Proof Demonstrate that the people behind Pantry Panic actually understand the problems
   they’re solving because they experience them themselves.
4. Shareability Articles should contain observations that make readers think: “That’s exactly what
   happens to me.”

The goal is not to aggressively sell Pantry Panic.

The goal is for readers to enjoy the article and leave with a positive impression of the brand
behind it.

⸻

## Tone of Voice

**Core personality**

Write as:

- an experienced engineer
- who is slightly annoyed by modern software
- but still fascinated by technology
- and capable of laughing at the absurdity of everyday life

The voice should feel:

- human
- confident
- observant
- witty
- slightly sarcastic
- occasionally combative

The voice should NOT feel:

- corporate
- salesy
- motivational
- overly enthusiastic
- “startup founder on LinkedIn”

⸻

## Humor

Humor comes from observations.

Not from punchlines.

Not from memes.

Not from excessive exaggeration.

Good:

> Somehow every supermarket can instantly locate twenty-seven varieties of hummus, yet remains
> incapable of placing them in a location that makes any logical sense.

Bad:

> HUMMUS. EVERYWHERE. CHAOS. PANIC.

The reader should smile because the observation is true.

⸻

## Writing Style

**Preferred**

Write like an opinion column or essay.

Use complete paragraphs.

Develop ideas.

Follow arguments through to a conclusion.

Allow thoughts to breathe.

**Avoid**

Do not write like modern social media copy:

Bad:

> Here’s the thing.
>
> Software is broken.
>
> Really broken.
>
> Let me explain.

Bad:

> Milk.
>
> Eggs.
>
> Bread.
>
> Chaos.

Bad:

> One notification.
>
> Then another.
>
> Then another.

This style should only be used very sparingly for emphasis.

⸻

## Structure

Every article should contain:

**Title**

Strong opinion, observation or question.

Examples:

- Why Grocery Apps Keep Getting Worse
- The Strange Satisfaction of Crossing Things Off a List
- We Accidentally Turned WhatsApp Into Everything

**Introduction**

1–2 paragraphs introducing the core idea.

**2–4 Subheadings**

Use meaningful H2 headings.

Not generic headings.

Good:

> ### The Internet Lost the Plot
>
> ### Every App Wants Your Attention
>
> ### Completion Feels Good Because Life Rarely Ends

Bad:

> ### Introduction
>
> ### Main Content
>
> ### Conclusion

End with a final observation.

Avoid:

- summaries
- key takeaways
- action items
- calls to action

The ending should feel like the final line of a good conversation.

⸻

## Length

Target:

- 500–900 words

Ideal:

- 600–800 words

Paragraphs should usually be:

- 3–8 sentences
- occasionally longer

Avoid excessive whitespace.

The article should feel like an actual article, not a sequence of one-liners.

⸻

## SEO Guidelines

SEO should be invisible.

Never write for search engines first.

Write for humans first.

However:

- include target keywords naturally
- use descriptive titles
- use meaningful headings
- answer real questions people have

A reader should never think:

This article was written to rank on Google.

⸻

## Topics That Fit Pantry Panic

Everyday Grocery Life

- shopping habits
- family coordination
- forgotten groceries
- meal planning
- supermarket behavior
- shared lists

Modern Software Frustrations

- subscriptions
- ads
- AI everywhere
- bloated apps
- dark patterns
- account creation

Technology

- self-hosting
- open source
- privacy
- ownership
- software quality

Human Behavior

- procrastination
- organization
- checklists
- habits
- decision fatigue
- why people forget things

⸻

Topics That Do Not Fit

Avoid:

- generic productivity advice
- life hacks
- hustle culture
- entrepreneurship content
- startup content
- growth marketing content
- AI hype pieces

If the article could be published on LinkedIn without anybody noticing it came from a grocery app
company, it is probably the wrong topic.

⸻

The Pantry Panic Test

Before publishing, ask:

1. Does this sound like a human wrote it?
2. Would somebody read this even if Pantry Panic didn’t exist?
3. Does this reinforce the Pantry Panic brand?
4. Is the humor based on truth rather than punchlines?
5. Are the paragraphs substantial enough to feel like an article?
6. Would I actually enjoy reading this myself?

If the answer to any of these questions is “no”, rewrite it.
