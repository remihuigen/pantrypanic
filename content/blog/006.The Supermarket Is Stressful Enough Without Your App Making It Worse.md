For reasons I don’t fully understand, software companies have become obsessed with turning simple activities into experiences.

Opening a banking app is now an experience.

Booking a flight is an experience.

Ordering food is an experience.

Apparently buying onions also needs to be an experience.

I’d personally settle for competence.

## Shopping Is Already Doing Quite Enough

The modern supermarket already provides more than enough opportunities for confusion. Half the products look identical, the store layout changes every six months, and the one item you’re looking for always seems to be located in the exact opposite corner of the building from where logic suggests it should be.

This is not a situation that benefits from additional complexity. Yet many apps seem determined to contribute.

You open the app and immediately encounter a popup. Then a feature announcement. Then a subscription reminder. Then a request to rate the app. Then a recommendation engine that has decided your recent purchase of pasta indicates a deep personal interest in carbohydrates.

Only after navigating this obstacle course do you finally reach your grocery list. It’s a strange outcome.

The software was created to simplify shopping, yet interacting with the software has become one of the more complicated parts of the shopping process.

## Engagement Is Not the Same as Helping

Somewhere along the way, product teams started optimizing for metrics instead of outcomes. Engagement became more important than efficiency. Session length became more important than task completion. The result is software that constantly demands attention instead of quietly helping people accomplish things.

The irony is that the best tools are often the least memorable.

Nobody reminisces about their calculator app. Nobody writes emotional LinkedIn posts about their note-taking widget. These products succeed because they solve a problem and then immediately get out of the way.

That’s an underrated quality in software.

## Effortless Usually Takes Effort

A lot of engineering effort goes into making things feel effortless. Fast loading times. Reliable synchronization. Predictable behavior. These aren’t flashy features. They don’t generate headlines. They’re just the foundation that allows everything else to work.

When they’re absent, however, everybody notices.

A grocery list app shouldn’t require concentration. It shouldn’t demand attention. It shouldn’t introduce itself every time you open it.

It should behave like a shopping cart. You don’t spend your grocery trip admiring the engineering of the cart. You simply expect it to follow along without becoming the main character.

Software should aspire to the same standard. The best user experience is often the one that disappears entirely.

Unfortunately that’s much harder to market than AI-powered shopping intelligence.
