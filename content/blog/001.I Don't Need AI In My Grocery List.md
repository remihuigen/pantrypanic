Every software category eventually discovers AI and immediately loses its mind.

Photo editors got AI. Fair enough. Writing tools got AI. That makes sense too. Then somebody looked at grocery lists and apparently concluded that what humanity really needs is a machine-learning model capable of predicting our future banana consumption.

This feels like a solution desperately searching for a problem.

## The Problem Was Never That Milk Is Hard To Predict

The average person doesn’t struggle because they forgot that milk exists. They struggle because they’re standing in a supermarket after work, they’re tired, they’re hungry, and they somehow failed to write down half the things they needed. No amount of artificial intelligence is going to fix the fact that Dave remembered toilet paper approximately six seconds after leaving the house.

What fascinates me is how often software companies confuse complexity with value. They start with a simple product that solves a real problem. Then they add recommendations, analytics, dashboards, assistants, insights, optimization engines and enough buzzwords to trigger a venture capitalist from three rooms away.

Eventually the original purpose of the app becomes secondary to all the exciting things built around it.

## Every Useful App Eventually Wants To Become A Platform

The grocery list becomes a platform.

The note-taking app becomes a workspace.

The calendar becomes a productivity operating system.

The user, meanwhile, is still trying to buy milk.

There seems to be an industry-wide belief that software must constantly become more sophisticated. In reality, many products become useful the moment you start removing things. Every button, popup and recommendation introduces friction. Every feature competes for attention. Every clever idea creates another opportunity for the software to get in your way.

## Grocery Shopping Is Already Absurd Enough

This is especially true for grocery shopping, which is already a mildly absurd activity. You enter the supermarket with a plan, get distracted by a discount sign, forget why you’re in aisle seven and leave with three items you didn’t need. The process hardly requires additional complexity.

Sometimes the most advanced technology is the technology you barely notice.

If somebody ever builds an AI capable of finding the item I’m looking for in a supermarket that has rearranged its entire floor plan for the third time this year, I’ll happily use it.

Until then, I think we’re probably okay with a list.
