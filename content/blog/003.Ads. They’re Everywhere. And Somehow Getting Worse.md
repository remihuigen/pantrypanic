# Ads. They’re Everywhere. And Somehow Getting Worse.

There was a time when advertisements had the decency to stay in designated advertising areas.

You’d see a billboard next to a road. A commercial during a television program. Maybe an ad in a newspaper if you happened to be the sort of person who still read newspapers.

The arrangement was simple. The ads existed. You ignored them. Everybody moved on with their lives.

Then the internet happened.

## The Arms Race For Human Attention

At first, the situation seemed manageable. Websites had banners. Sometimes they flashed aggressively and attempted to convince you that local singles were interested in meeting you despite overwhelming evidence to the contrary. It wasn’t ideal, but it was survivable.

Unfortunately, the advertising industry interpreted the existence of ad blockers not as a sign that people disliked ads, but as a challenge.

The result has been a decades-long arms race between ordinary people trying to complete basic tasks and marketers attempting to insert themselves into every available square centimeter of human attention.

Open a news article and you’re greeted by a popup asking you to subscribe before you’ve even determined whether the article is worth reading.

Watch a video and you’re forced through multiple advertisements for products you will never buy.

Use a free app and half the screen is dedicated to convincing you that one weird trick can dramatically improve your tax situation.

Even your refrigerator is probably trying to monetize something at this point.

## Ads Have Become Too Ambitious

What makes modern advertising so exhausting isn’t necessarily the quantity. It’s the ambition.

Ads no longer want to inform you that a product exists. They want to follow you around the internet for three weeks because you briefly glanced at a frying pan once.

You look at a vacuum cleaner. Suddenly every platform becomes convinced that vacuum cleaners are your defining personality trait.

Instagram knows.

Google knows.

Facebook knows.

The vacuum cleaner knows.

It’s difficult not to admire the commitment.

## Software Gets Away With Very Weird Behavior

The strange thing is that many companies seem genuinely surprised when users react negatively to this. Entire product experiences are sacrificed to create additional advertising opportunities, and then executives wonder why people describe their apps as annoying.

Imagine a restaurant interrupting your meal every four minutes to recommend another restaurant. Nobody would tolerate it. Yet software somehow gets away with behavior that would be considered deeply strange in almost any other context.

Perhaps the most frustrating part is that advertisements often make products worse for the people who actually use them. Interfaces become cluttered. Performance suffers. Features are designed around monetization opportunities rather than user needs.

The app still technically solves the original problem, but only after you’ve navigated a maze of interruptions specifically engineered to monetize your existence.

## Products Are Becoming Ad Delivery Systems

And that’s the real issue. Advertising used to fund products. Increasingly, products seem to exist to deliver advertising.

That’s a subtle distinction, but an important one. One creates value and pays for it with ads. The other creates ads and tolerates users because somebody has to look at them.

Unfortunately, the second model appears to be winning. At least until somebody figures out how to put advertisements inside advertisements.

Which, given current trends, probably isn’t far away.
