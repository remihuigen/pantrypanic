There appears to be a universal law governing households.

The moment one person enters a supermarket, everybody else suddenly remembers everything they’ve ever needed.

This phenomenon is remarkably consistent.

You can spend an entire week asking whether anything needs to be added to the shopping list. The answer will be no. Everybody is satisfied. Supplies are abundant. Society is functioning normally.

Then you arrive at the supermarket. Within minutes, new requirements begin emerging from seemingly nowhere.

Milk.

Bananas.

Coffee.

Dishwasher tablets.

That one ingredient required for a recipe nobody mentioned until this exact moment.

## Nobody Is Thinking About Yogurt Availability

It’s tempting to blame the people sending these updates, but honestly, I think they’re victims of the same system.

The human brain is terrible at inventory management.

Nobody spends their afternoon thinking about paper towels. Nobody sits at work reflecting on yogurt availability. These facts only become relevant when somebody opens a cupboard and discovers that reality has diverged significantly from expectations.

The timing isn’t malicious. It’s inevitable.

## The Shopper Is Playing On Hard Mode

What fascinates me is how this creates a strange asymmetry between the people at home and the person doing the shopping. The people at home experience the grocery trip as a continuous opportunity to provide useful information. The shopper experiences it as a live-service game receiving balance updates during active gameplay. Every notification has the potential to invalidate previous decisions. You carefully planned a route through the store. You optimized aisle order. You developed momentum.

Then somebody remembers toothpaste.

The route collapses.

Back to aisle two.

## Reality Keeps Filing Bug Reports

This isn’t a serious problem, obviously. Humanity has survived much worse than dynamic grocery requirements. Yet it’s one of those tiny frustrations that everybody immediately recognizes because it happens so often.

Part of the reason grocery shopping remains surprisingly difficult is that it’s one of the few activities where planning and reality constantly collide.

You create a list based on assumptions.

Then reality starts filing bug reports.

The recipe requires more ingredients than expected.

The fridge contains less cheese than expected.

The household consumes coffee at a rate previously believed impossible.

New information appears continuously.

## A Grocery List Is Really A Sync System

A good grocery list isn’t really a plan. It’s a synchronization mechanism between multiple imperfect humans attempting to coordinate domestic life.

When viewed through that lens, the constant updates become slightly less annoying.

Slightly.

I’m still going to complain when somebody remembers six additional items while I’m standing in the checkout queue.

Some traditions deserve preservation.
